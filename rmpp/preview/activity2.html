Reflective Activity 2

Case Study - Inappropriate Use of Surveys: Cambridge Analytica (2018)

Jump to Section Overview What Happened Why It Was Used Ethical & Social Implications Legal & Regulatory Impact Professional Responsibilities Lessons Learned Conclusion

1. Overview

The Cambridge Analytica scandal (Confessore, New York Times, 2018) represents one of the clearest examples of surveys being used for purposes far beyond the participants’ understanding. What appeared to be an innocent Facebook personality quiz became a large-scale data-harvesting operation that influenced political campaigns across the United States and the United Kingdom.

2. What Actually Happened

A Cambridge academic, Dr. Aleksandr Kogan, created a Facebook app called This Is Your Digital Life. Around 270,000 users were paid to complete a psychological survey linked to their Facebook accounts. Because of Facebook’s API design at that time, the app also accessed information about each participant’s friends, names, Likes, locations, and other personal details—resulting in data from up to 87 million people being collected without consent.

The data were sold to Cambridge Analytica, a political consulting firm that used it to build psychographic profiles for targeted political advertising during the 2016 US Presidential Election and the UK Brexit referendum.

3. Why It Was Used

Surveys provide structured, interpretable features—such as personality traits and attitudes—that can feed predictive models. By linking survey results to Facebook Likes and metadata, Cambridge Analytica could infer users’ openness, extroversion, or political leaning, enabling precise behavioural advertising.

4. Ethical and Social Implications

Perspective Key Issues Discussion
Ethical Informed consent violated Only direct participants consented; their friends did not. Consent was deceptive, as users were told the data were for “academic research.”
Purpose limitation ignored Data collected for research were sold for political marketing—classic function creep.
Respect for autonomy undermined People were profiled and targeted without knowledge or control.
Social Manipulation and mistrust Psychographic targeting reinforced polarisation and eroded public trust in social media and research surveys.
Erosion of privacy norms Users realised that ordinary online quizzes could expose sensitive psychological information.

5. Legal and Regulatory Impact

  • Facebook was fined $5 billion (FTC, 2019) in the US for deceptive privacy practices.
  • The UK Information Commissioner’s Office imposed a £500,000 fine under the pre-GDPR Data Protection Act 1998.
  • Under GDPR (Article 5), such behaviour breaches principles of lawfulness, fairness, transparency, and purpose limitation.

These enforcement actions marked a turning point in global data-protection awareness.

6. Professional Responsibilities of Data Scientists

  • Respect privacy and obtain valid, informed consent.
  • Avoid deception and secondary use of data without permission.
  • Ensure transparency of purpose and processing.
  • Prioritise the public good over organisational gain.

The Cambridge Analytica case shows what happens when these duties are ignored: trust collapses, reputations are destroyed, and entire platforms face regulatory scrutiny.

7. Lessons Learned

  1. Never disguise data-collection intent.
  2. Minimise data; never collect information about third parties without consent.
  3. Separate research from marketing—repurposing requires renewed consent.
  4. Embed ethical review for every survey-based project.

8. Conclusion

Cambridge Analytica transformed a simple survey into a political-profiling machine. It demonstrates how weak consent, unclear purpose, and profit motives can turn legitimate research into manipulation. For data scientists, responsible data handling is not optional—it is foundational to trustworthy analytics and sustainable public confidence.
